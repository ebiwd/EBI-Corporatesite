Open Targets
## A groundbreaking public-private partnership [TODO: make this an accordian fold out to track engagement] Open Targets is a unique, large-scale, public-private partnership that uses human genetics and genomics data for systematic drug target identification and prioritisation. ### The challenge Only five in 5000 drugs that enter preclinical testing progress to the human testing phase, and only one in 5000 makes it to the market. On average, the process takes twelve years and billions of dollars. One of the biggest barriers in drug discovery today is selecting the right drug target at the beginning of the pipeline the first place. ### The solution To overcome this challenge, experts from academia and the pharmaceutical sector came together to create Open Targets. The partnership brings together expertise, skills, evidence and technologies from seven complementary institutions. The aim? Transform drug discovery by systematically identifying and prioritising targets from which safe and effective medicines can be developed. ### Open Targets is a partnership The current scientific focus of Open Targets includes oncology, immunology and neurodegeneration, but the R&D framework developed can be applied to all aspects of human disease. ### The outcomes Open Targets openly shares its data, software and results with the scientific community. Since its launch, it has released two essential decision-making systems for drug target identification: - Open Targets Platform - find and prioritise new targets for drug discovery - Open Targets Genetics - explore variant-gene-trait associations from UK Biobank and GWAS Catalog If we ever hope to understand disease mechanisms and improve the existing one in 5000 success rate for drugs making it to the market, pre-competitive collaborations such as Open Targets, which bridge the gap between academia and industry, are essential. There is still substantial scope for similar collaborations to help us tackle grand challenges, including other phases of the drug discovery pipeline, crop production, antimicrobial resistance and so much more.
